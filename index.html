The plain-English guide

What is an SSSP, and do you need one in NZ?

A Site-Specific Safety Plan (SSSP) is a document that sets out how the health and safety risks of one particular job or site will be managed. It pulls together the hazards on that site, the controls for them, the safe method for the work, the PPE required, and who's responsible — so everyone arriving on site knows how the job will be run safely.

Is it legally required?

Not as a named form. Under the Health and Safety at Work Act 2015, every business (a "PCBU") has a duty to manage its work risks so far as is reasonably practicable. An SSSP isn't written into the Act by name — but it's the standard, widely recognised way to demonstrate you're meeting that duty, and main contractors and principals almost always require one contractually before they'll let you start on site. In practice, no SSSP often means no job.

SSSP vs SWMS — what's the difference?

An SSSP is the whole-of-site plan. A Safe Work Method Statement (SWMS), sometimes called a job safety analysis or task analysis, zooms in on a single task and breaks it into steps, listing the hazards and controls for each. A good SSSP usually contains one or more SWMS for the higher-risk parts of the work. CompliAI drafts both.

What makes a plan "site-specific"?

The clue is in the name. A generic template that could describe any site is exactly what principals are trying to avoid. A real SSSP names the actual hazards on this site — the power lines on that boundary, the occupied house next door, the gas bottles in that corner. That's the part CompliAI speeds up: you describe the real site, and the draft reflects it, instead of you starting from a blank or generic template.

General guidance only, not legal advice. Always check current requirements with WorkSafe New Zealand and your principal contractor, and review every plan against your actual site.
